Subject: SigilGraph v0.8.0 launch – post-quantum, genesis-to-tip light client at ~7k headers/s, memory-bounded chain, and more

We are launching SigilGraph v0.8.0, a major milestone for the post-quantum, Flux-native blockchain with a microsecond light client. The testnet (network sigil-g0, ~975,000 blocks and climbing) is live and all numbers below are reproducible with curl and a browser.

Key numbers first:

- Genesis-to-tip verifiable header sync: ~7,000 headers/second on a live 4‑peer mesh. A light client that only needs headers (bincode‑encoded, ~20× smaller than full‑block JSON) downloads, verifies, and persists the entire chain as a contiguous prefix, resumable on restart.
- In‑browser post‑quantum key generation: ML‑DSA‑87 (Dilithium5) + ML‑KEM‑1024 (Kyber1024) hybridized with Ed25519 completes in ~267 ms with no WASM – pure JavaScript via @noble/post‑quantum.
- A single ~2,568‑byte proof of the entire chain: an Ajtai/SIS flux‑fold lattice folding scheme, transparent, post‑quantum, no trusted setup, binding N block commitments into one constant‑size proof. A late‑joiner verifies one fold of the prefix, adopts the checkpoint, applies live blocks – divergence zero.
- Memory‑bounded chain: the block producer runs in constant RAM regardless of chain length. Blocks live in an append‑only on‑disk log; an in‑RAM sliding window of the latest 8,192 blocks plus a streaming recovery that rebuilds state from the log one block at a time keeps working‑set RAM flat.
- Committed‑in‑roots emission: 5 SIGIL per block, halving every 2,100,000 blocks, geometrically capped at 21 million. A fail‑loud band enforcer reads the committed wallet‑state root and halts the chain if realized supply drifts by more than ±2%.
- Verified‑identity earning gate: a wallet binds an identity by signing a domain‑separated, nonce’d message. Verified wallets earn to themselves; unverified miners’ rewards route to the dev‑fee treasury. All transfers are verified non‑negative.
- M‑of‑N quorum with an LLM‑debate policy layer: autonomous‑agent money actions require agreement from two independent language‑model agents under a cryptographic quorum, with security bounded by a P(bad)=f^K eclipse model.

Design details that make the light‑client sync robust

- Transport is a libp2p request‑response channel (point‑to‑point), not gossip. The server rate‑limits full‑block serves; the light client requests only headers. Each header carries the four committed state roots (wallet, dex, event log, contract) plus the fields the tip‑proof binds.
- The client recomputes a BLAKE3 tip‑proof fingerprint over every header’s fields and roots, then advances a contiguous height pointer on disk. A restart resumes from the verified prefix without re‑downloading; out‑of‑order chunks fill gaps without ever reporting a non‑contiguous height as synced.
- Peers that fail to serve a requested range are benched after two consecutive misses, with short timeouts, so one slow node cannot stall fan‑out. With a single healthy node the client still sustains thousands of headers per second.

Measured on the live mesh: 73,731 verified headers downloaded and stored in ~10.6 seconds (wire‑bound; local verification runs at ~26,000 headers/second). The producer serves these headers without paging the whole chain into RAM; working‑set RAM stays in the tens of MB as the on‑disk log grows, and a full restart stream‑recovers ~102,000 blocks in bounded memory.

The chain’s post‑quantum identity layer runs entirely in a browser tab: hybrid Ed25519 + Dilithium5 + Kyber1024 keygen in ~267 ms, FIPS 203 / FIPS 204 NIST level 5. The emission schedule and cap are independent, compile‑time verified constants: a const_assert confirms the geometric sum hits exactly 21 million, and the runtime enforcer halts the chain if on‑chain supply ever exceeds that cap or drifts beyond the ±2% band.
